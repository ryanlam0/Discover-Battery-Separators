POSTER STORY GUIDE
==================
How to tell the story, which plots to use, and what to say.


THE RESEARCH QUESTION
---------------------
"Can we predict whether a polymer is thermally safe for use as a battery
separator based on its material properties — and can we screen untested
polymers to discover new candidates?"


THE ONE-SENTENCE PITCH
----------------------
"We trained a KNN classifier on known battery separator polymers to predict
thermal safety, then screened 101 untested polymers and found 5 strong
candidates that have never been tested as separators."


STORY ARC (follow this order on the poster)
--------------------------------------------

1. HOOK (top-left, ~2 sentences)
   "Every lithium-ion battery has a thin polymer film — the separator —
   that prevents the electrodes from touching and causing a fire. If this
   film melts or shrinks during overheating, the battery can catch fire.
   Which polymers are thermally safe, and can we find new ones?"

   WHY IT WORKS: Everyone uses batteries. Fire is scary. Sets up the stakes.


2. DATA COLLECTION (left side, brief)
   Explain the 3 data sources and how they were joined:
   - OpenPoly database: 741 polymers with material properties (Tg, Tm, Td,
     tensile strength, Young's modulus, elongation at break)
   - Celgard: web scraped 30 commercial separator product specs
   - Literature: web scraped 337 rows from 7 PMC review articles for
     separator-specific properties (porosity, ionic conductivity)
   - Joined by polymer name → 39 labeled training polymers

   PLOT: None needed. A small data flow diagram or table is fine.

   RUBRIC: Hits "Data Collection" (web scraping + multi-source joins)


3. SAFETY LABELS (brief text)
   "We labeled 39 known separator polymers as safe (maintains structural
   integrity above 150C) or unsafe (fails at or below 150C) based on
   published thermal stability data."

   Training set: 25 safe, 14 unsafe


4. MODEL BUILDING & SELECTION (center)
   PLOT: model_selection.png
   - Shows F1 score vs k for both Euclidean and Manhattan distance
   - Best point labeled: k=7, Euclidean, F1 = 0.812

   WHAT TO SAY: "We built a pipeline with StandardScaler + KNN classifier
   and used GridSearchCV with 5-fold cross-validation to tune k and the
   distance metric. The best model uses k=7 neighbors with Euclidean
   distance."

   RUBRIC: Hits "Data Analysis" (pipeline, StandardScaler, GridSearchCV,
   cross-validation)


5. MODEL EVALUATION (center-right)
   PLOT: confusion_matrix_cv.png (USE THE CV VERSION, NOT TRAINING)
   - Shows cross-validation confusion matrix: 23+10 correct, 4+2 errors
   - Per-class metrics table: safe F1=0.885, unsafe F1=0.769
   - Cross-Val Macro F1 = 0.827

   WHAT TO SAY: "The cross-validation confusion matrix shows out-of-fold
   predictions — each polymer is predicted by a model that never saw it
   during training. The model catches 71% of unsafe polymers (10 out of 14).
   In safety-critical applications, missing a dangerous polymer is worse
   than rejecting a safe one."

   KEY INSIGHT: Unsafe recall (0.714) is the most important metric here.
   5 unsafe polymers were missed — these would be dangerous false positives
   in a real application.

   RUBRIC: Hits "Data Analysis" (confusion matrix, precision, recall, F1)
   and "Real-World Application" (safety-critical framing)


6. CLUSTERING (bottom-left)
   PLOTS: elbow_plot.png + clusters_vs_safety.png

   WHAT TO SAY: "We used K-means clustering (k=3, chosen from elbow plot)
   to see if polymers naturally group by their material properties. Cluster 2
   is 90% unsafe, Cluster 1 is 82% safe — material properties partially
   predict safety, but the overlap shows a supervised classifier is needed."

   KEY INSIGHT: Unsupervised clustering alone isn't enough. The KNN
   classifier adds value by learning the decision boundary.

   RUBRIC: Hits "Data Analysis" (K-means clustering, unsupervised learning)


7. SCREENING RESULTS (bottom-center)
   PLOT: screening_results.png

   WHAT TO SAY: "We screened 101 untested polymers from the OpenPoly
   database. The model predicted 55 as safe and 46 as unsafe. Most
   engineering polymers are thermally stable — the model's value is
   identifying the ~45% that aren't, narrowing the pool for further testing."

   RUBRIC: Hits "Real-World Application" (actionable screening)


8. TOP CANDIDATES & CONCLUSION (bottom-right)
   PLOTS: top_candidates.png + candidate_properties.png + known_separator_properties.png

   WHAT TO SAY: "5 polymers were predicted safe with 100% confidence:
   polyimides, PEKK, PGA, PEN, and PA 6. These have high glass transition
   temperatures (>300K), high melting points (>490K), and strong mechanical
   properties — making them promising candidates for separator testing."

   known_separator_properties.png shows the same 6 material properties
   for 10 well-known separator polymers (PP, PVDF, PAN, PET, PI, PPS,
   PEI, PA6, PLA, PTFE) on the same axes as candidate_properties.png.
   Place them side by side so viewers can directly compare candidates to
   established separators and see that the candidates' properties fall in
   the same range — or exceed — those of proven materials.

   LIMITATION: "Our model uses only the 6 material properties available
   in the OpenPoly database. Separator-specific properties like porosity
   and ionic conductivity were only available for the 39 known separators
   (from literature scraping) — the 101 screening candidates have never
   been tested as separators, so those measurements don't exist for them.
   This is why we built the model on universally available features only."

   CONCLUSION: "Material properties alone can predict thermal safety with
   ~83% accuracy. Our model narrows 101 untested polymers down to 5 strong
   candidates, but real-world lab testing for porosity, ionic conductivity,
   and electrochemical stability is still needed before these polymers
   could be used in batteries."

   RUBRIC: Hits "Storytelling" (clear narrative arc from problem to solution)
   and "Real-World Application" (specific, named candidates)

QUESTIONS YOU SHOULD BE READY TO ANSWER
---------------------------------------
Q: "Why KNN and not logistic regression or random forest?"
A: "KNN was taught in class and works well for small datasets. It makes
   no assumptions about the decision boundary shape."

Q: "Why only 39 training polymers?"
A: "We could only find 39 polymers that have both published safety data AND
   material properties in the OpenPoly database. Small but representative."

Q: "Why is unsafe recall only 71%?"
A: "With only 14 unsafe examples, the model has limited data to learn from.
   More labeled data would improve recall."

Q: "Are these candidates actually good for batteries?"
A: "We predict thermal safety only — whether the polymer won't melt/shrink.
   A good separator also needs porosity, ionic conductivity, and chemical
   stability, which require lab testing."

Q: "Why did you drop porosity and conductivity from the model?"
A: "Those features were only available for training polymers (from literature).
   Screening candidates don't have that data, so filling with medians would
   bias predictions. Using only the 6 universally available properties is
   more honest."

Q: "What do the clusters represent?"
A: "K-means grouped polymers by similarity in their material properties,
   without knowing safety labels. The fact that clusters partially align
   with safety shows material properties contain safety-relevant signal."

Q: "Why k=3 clusters if you only have 2 safety classes?"
A: "We chose k=3 from the elbow plot — it's where inertia stops dropping
   sharply. The clusters represent natural material groupings, not safety
   classes. That they partially align is the interesting finding."
