POSTER STORY GUIDE

Organized by the required poster template sections.

TITLE: "Don't Let Your Next iPhone Catch Fire!
        Discovering the Next Safe Lithium-Ion Battery Separator Polymers"


=====================================================================
SECTION 1: ABSTRACT
=====================================================================

"Every lithium-ion battery contains a thin polymer separator that
prevents the electrodes from short-circuiting. Separators can fail in
two ways: thermally (the film melts or shrinks during overheating,
allowing the electrodes to touch) or mechanically (lithium dendrites —
tiny needle-like metal growths that form during charging — puncture
through a separator that isn't stiff or strong enough). Both failure
modes cause short circuits that can lead to thermal runaway and fire.

Most commercial separators use polyethylene (PE) or polypropylene (PP),
which melt at just 130-165C. During thermal runaway — triggered by
overcharging, physical damage, or manufacturing defects — internal
temperatures can spike well past 200C, causing these separators to
fail. This is the mechanism behind major battery recalls (e.g., Samsung
Galaxy Note 7) and EV battery fires.

The industry needs separators made from polymers with better thermal
AND mechanical stability, but experimentally testing every candidate is
expensive and slow — each polymer must be fabricated into a film,
assembled into a test cell, and put through abuse testing. We trained a
KNN classifier on 39 known separator polymers to predict safety from
6 material properties (3 thermal, 3 mechanical), then screened 101
untested polymers and identified 5 strong candidates. Our model achieves
a cross-validated macro F1 of 0.827 and narrows the candidate pool from
101 to 5, replacing months of lab screening with seconds of computation."


=====================================================================
SECTION 2: RESEARCH QUESTION
=====================================================================

MOTIVATION: Current commercial separators (PE, PP) melt at 130-165C,
  making them the weakest link during thermal runaway. They are also
  vulnerable to dendrite puncture due to limited mechanical strength.
  Testing new polymer candidates in the lab is expensive and time-
  consuming. A predictive model can narrow the field before any lab
  work begins.

QUESTION: "Can we predict whether a polymer is thermally safe for use
  as a lithium-ion battery separator based on its material properties —
  and can we screen untested polymers to discover new candidates?"

SECTION 3: DATA COLLECTION
=====================================================================

DATA FLOW (chronological):

  Step 1: WEB SCRAPED PMC literature
    How: requests + BeautifulSoup on 7 open-access review articles
    What we got: which polymers have been tested as battery separators
      and their thermal stability (shutdown temperatures)
    How it helped: gave us 42 known separator polymers and the data
      to label each as safe (>150C) or unsafe (<=150C)

  Step 2: WEB SCRAPED Celgard product page + 30 PDF datasheets
    How: requests + BeautifulSoup for the product page, pdfplumber
      to parse the PDF datasheets
    What we got: commercial separator product specs (polymer type,
      porosity, thickness, tensile strength, puncture strength)
    How it helped: confirmed which polymers are used in real
      commercial separators (mainly PP and PE variants)

  Step 3: DOWNLOADED OpenPoly database (741 polymers)
    What we got: 6 material properties for each polymer (Tg, Tm, Td,
      tensile strength, Young's modulus, elongation at break)
    How it helped: provided the model features AND the 101 screening
      candidates (polymers not in our training set)

  Step 4: MERGED all three by polymer name
    42 known separators (from Steps 1-2) joined with OpenPoly
    properties (Step 3) → 39 polymers with complete data
    = our training set (25 safe, 14 unsafe)

  Safety threshold (150C) based on industry abuse test standards
  (UL 2054, IEC 62133).

SECTION 4: DATA EXPLORATION
=====================================================================

--- 4a: WHY THESE 6 PROPERTIES? ---

WHAT TO SAY:
  "We selected 6 material properties that directly relate to how a
  separator performs under thermal stress:"

  Thermal properties (will it survive overheating?):
    - Tg (Glass Transition Temp): temperature where the polymer goes
      from rigid to rubbery. Below Tg, the separator is stiff and
      stable. A higher Tg means it stays rigid at higher temperatures.
    - Tm (Melting Temp): temperature where the polymer melts and loses
      structural integrity. If Tm is too low, the separator melts
      during thermal runaway and the battery short-circuits.
    - Td (Decomposition Temp): temperature where the polymer chemically
      breaks down. Higher Td = more thermally stable.

  Mechanical properties (will it resist dendrite puncture?):
    - Tensile Strength: how much force it takes to pull the film apart.
      The separator must resist tearing during battery assembly and use.
    - Young's Modulus: stiffness of the material. A stiffer separator
      better resists lithium dendrite puncture — tiny needle-like metal
      growths that can pierce the separator during charging, causing a
      short circuit and thermal runaway.
    - Elongation at Break: how much it stretches before breaking. Some
      flexibility is needed to absorb mechanical stress, but too much
      means it deforms and thins out under heat.

  LABELS vs FEATURES: Our safety labels are based on shutdown
  temperature — the temperature at which a separator loses structural
  integrity (not just Tm or Td, but when it actually fails in testing).
  But the model predicts safety using all 6 properties, not just
  temperature. The model learns that certain combinations of thermal
  AND mechanical properties correlate with safe/unsafe — a polymer
  with high Tm but very low stiffness might still fail because it
  deforms under stress.

  KEY INSIGHT: Safety isn't just about heat. There are two failure
  pathways — thermal (separator melts/shrinks) and mechanical (dendrite
  punctures through). Our model captures both because it uses thermal
  AND mechanical features. For example, PI (polyimide) is safe primarily
  due to extreme heat resistance (Tg=554K, Td=743K), while PAN is safe
  primarily due to mechanical rigidity (Young's Modulus=6110 MPa). Both
  are proven separators, but for different reasons.

  WHY ONLY THESE 6: These are the only properties available in the
  OpenPoly database for both training and screening polymers.
  Separator-specific properties (porosity, ionic conductivity) were
  only available for the 39 known separators from literature scraping,
  not for the 101 screening candidates.


--- 4b: CLUSTERING ---

WHAT TO SAY:
  "We used K-means clustering (k=3, chosen from elbow plot) to see if
  polymers naturally group by their material properties. Cluster 2 is
  90% unsafe, Cluster 1 is 82% safe — material properties partially
  predict safety, but the overlap shows a supervised classifier is
  needed."

KEY INSIGHT: Unsupervised clustering alone isn't enough. The fact that
  clusters partially align with safety labels shows that material
  properties contain safety-relevant signal, but a supervised classifier
  (KNN) is needed to learn the actual decision boundary.

--- 5c: SCREENING & TOP CANDIDATES ---

WHAT TO SAY:
  "We screened 101 untested polymers from the OpenPoly database. The
  model predicted 55 as safe and 46 as unsafe. 5 polymers were predicted
  safe with 100% confidence: polyimides, PEKK, PGA, PEN, and PA 6."

RADAR CHART ANALYSIS (radar_candidates.png):
  The radar chart compares all 5 candidates against PI (polyimide), a
  known high-performance separator, across all 6 normalized properties.

  Closest to PI (strongest candidates for lab testing):
    - polyimides: Nearly identical to PI across all 6 properties (<7%
      difference on every axis). This is essentially the same polymer
      family — highest confidence pick.
    - PEKK: Very close on thermal properties (Tm, Td) and tensile
      strength (<8% off), with a slightly higher Young's modulus. A
      strong second candidate.
    - PEN: Close on Tm and Td (<4% off), slightly lower Tg, but higher
      modulus. Another strong match.

  More different from PI (still safe, but different property profile):
    - PA 6: Much lower Tg (321K vs 554K) and much higher elongation at
      break (79% vs 11%). Thermally safe but mechanically very different
      — more flexible, less rigid. Worth testing but may behave
      differently as a separator film.
    - PGA: Lowest Tg and Td of the five, but extremely high tensile
      strength (156 MPa) and Young's modulus (5655 MPa). Very stiff
      and strong but less thermally robust. Could work in applications
      where mechanical strength matters more.

  WHAT THIS MEANS: Polymers closest to PI on the radar (polyimides,
  PEKK, PEN) are the safest bets for lab testing — they match a proven
  separator's property profile. PA 6 and PGA are still predicted safe
  but have different trade-offs (flexibility vs stiffness) that would
  need to be evaluated for specific battery designs.

SECTION 6: CONCLUSION
=====================================================================

WHAT TO SAY:
  "Material properties alone can predict thermal safety with ~83%
  accuracy. Our model narrows 101 untested polymers down to 5 strong
  candidates, saving significant lab screening time."

LIMITATION:
  "Our model uses only the 6 material properties available in the
  OpenPoly database. Separator-specific properties like porosity and
  ionic conductivity were only available for the 39 known separators
  (from literature scraping) — the 101 screening candidates have never
  been tested as separators, so those measurements don't exist. This is
  why we built the model on universally available features only."

FUTURE WORK:
  "Real-world lab testing for porosity, ionic conductivity, and
  electrochemical stability is still needed before these polymers could
  be used in batteries. More labeled training data would also improve
  unsafe recall beyond 71%."

QUESTIONS YOU SHOULD BE READY TO ANSWER
=====================================================================

Q: "Why KNN and not logistic regression or random forest?"
A: "KNN was taught in class and works well for small datasets. It makes
   no assumptions about the decision boundary shape."

Q: "Why only 39 training polymers?"
A: "We could only find 39 polymers that have both published safety data
   AND material properties in the OpenPoly database. Small but
   representative."

Q: "Why is unsafe recall only 71%?"
A: "With only 14 unsafe examples, the model has limited data to learn
   from. More labeled data would improve recall."

Q: "Are these candidates actually good for batteries?"
A: "We predict thermal safety only — whether the polymer won't
   melt/shrink. A good separator also needs porosity, ionic
   conductivity, and chemical stability, which require lab testing."

Q: "Why did you drop porosity and conductivity from the model?"
A: "Those features were only available for training polymers (from
   literature). The 101 screening candidates have never been tested as
   separators, so those measurements don't exist for them. Using only
   the 6 universally available properties lets us actually screen new
   polymers without biasing predictions with imputed medians."

Q: "What do the clusters represent?"
A: "K-means grouped polymers by similarity in their material properties,
   without knowing safety labels. The fact that clusters partially align
   with safety shows material properties contain safety-relevant signal."

Q: "Why k=3 clusters if you only have 2 safety classes?"
A: "We chose k=3 from the elbow plot — it's where inertia stops dropping
   sharply. The clusters represent natural material groupings, not safety
   classes. That they partially align is the interesting finding."
